Commodities

Commodities are an important aspect of Americans' daily lives, providing the food they eat and the energy used to propel their cars. A commodity is a basic good traded in large volumes and interchangeable with other goods of the same type. Commodities are either for immediate delivery in spot trading or for conveyance later when traded as futures. Commodity markets deal in metals (aluminum, copper, gold, lead, nickel, silver, zinc, etc.) and “soft” items (cocoa, coffee, sugar, oil, etc.).

Key Takeaways
  • Commodities are broadly categorized into metals, energy, livestock and meat, and agricultural products.
  • Investing in commodities offers a way to diversify investment portfolios beyond conventional securities
  • Commodity investments are considered risky due to their susceptibility to unpredictable events like weather, epidemics, and disasters.
  • Investors have various avenues for investing in commodities, including futures contracts, options, and exchange-traded funds (ETFs).
  • Commodities serve as a diversification tool for investors as their prices often move inversely to stocks, offering potential returns during market volatility.
History of Commodities Trading

Commodities trading has ancient origins, predating the trade in stocks, bonds, and even money, according to some anthropologists. The ability to establish sophisticated trading systems and facilitate commodity exchange played a crucial role in the rise of empires and colonialism.

Before the advent of efficient transportation like railroads and steamships in the 19th century, only high-value goods like spices, coffee, cocoa, gold, and silver were traded across continents due to the prohibitive cost of transportation. However, significant advancements in transportation technology, such as railways and refrigerated train cars, in the 19th century, and the development of large bulk carriers and harbor facilities post-World War II, drastically reduced shipping costs, making global trade more accessible.

By some estimates, the cost of shipping bulk goods decreased by about 90% between the 1870s and a century later.

As evident from this discussion, commodities trading is not only steeped in tradition but also embraces cutting-edge technology to enhance global trade volumes. Commodities trading occurs through exchanges, which serve as both physical locations for trading activities and legal entities responsible for enforcing standardized commodity contracts and associated investment products.

In recent years, exchanges have undergone consolidation, similar to other industries. While many exchanges offer a variety of commodities, some specialize in specific types. In the United States, notable exchanges include the Chicago Mercantile Exchange (CME), the New York Mercantile Exchange, and the Intercontinental Exchange based in Atlanta.

  • The commodities market operates based on the principles of supply and demand, similar to any other marketplace.
  • Shortages in commodity availability led to increased demand and subsequently higher prices.
  • Unexpected events such as disease outbreaks or adverse weather conditions can disrupt commodity supply chains, resulting in sudden spikes in demand.
  • Global economic trends and technological advancements greatly influence the commodities market.
  • The industrial growth in countries like China and India has significantly raised the demand for metals, impacting their availability worldwide.
What Influences Commodity Prices?
  • Cost Fluctuations: Changes in expenses like storage and financing affect commodity prices.
  • Currency Shifts: Alterations in the value of currencies, especially the U.S. dollar, impact commodity prices globally.
  • Global Stability: Political tensions or economic instability in key regions can disrupt commodity supply chains, driving prices up.
  • Economic Conditions: The health of the global economy influences demand for commodities, with growth boosting demand and downturns reducing it.
  • Government Actions: Policies such as tariffs or subsidies can directly affect commodity prices.
  • Inflation and Interest Rates: Commodities are often used to hedge against inflation, while interest rate changes influence investment costs.
  • Speculation: Trading based on future price expectations can lead to price swings in commodities.
  • Logistics Costs: Expenses related to storage and transportation contribute to commodity pricing.
  • Supply and Demand: The balance between what's available and what's wanted drives commodity prices.
  • Technological Progress: Innovations can change production costs or create demand for new materials.
  • Weather and Environmental Factors: Natural disasters like floods or extreme weather events can disrupt commodity production, affecting prices.
  • Using Futures to Invest in Commodities
    • Futures Contracts: Legal agreements to buy or sell commodities at set prices and times.
    • Buyers and Sellers: Buyers commit to purchasing the commodity, while sellers agree to deliver it when the contract ends.
    • Investor Types: Some use futures to protect against price changes, while others speculate for potential profit.
Commercial and Institutional Buyers of Futures
  • Smart Planning and Risk Control: Many businesses rely on futures contracts to manage financial risks and ensure stable operations. For example, airlines use them to keep fuel costs predictable, safeguarding their budgets from unpredictable price swings.
  • Guarding Against Price Surprises: Farming groups also turn to futures contracts to shield themselves from the uncertainty of crop prices. By setting prices in advance, they shield their incomes from unexpected market changes, providing stability for their operations.
Speculators in Commodity Futures
  • Savvy Investors: Speculators in commodity futures are often seasoned traders seeking short-term gains from market movements. They employ various tactics to profit from price shifts without physically owning the commodities.
  • Opening Trading Accounts: Individuals interested in commodity speculation may need to set up specialized trading accounts. This process involves acknowledging the risks and funding the accounts to cover potential losses.
  • Balancing Risk and Reward: While futures trading offers the potential for significant gains, it also carries substantial risks. Successful traders understand the importance of managing risks and have the experience to navigate volatile markets.
Advantages and Considerations
  • Straightforward Investment: Futures contracts offer a straightforward way to invest in commodities, focusing solely on price movements without the complexity of stock-specific factors.
  • Potential for Lucrative Returns: The leverage provided by futures contracts can amplify profits, even with a modest initial investment. However, it also increases the risk of losses, requiring careful risk management.
Futures Options: A Safer Approach
  • Risk Mitigation: Due to the high volatility of commodity markets, direct investment in futures contracts can be perilous, especially for novices. If a trade goes awry, losses can exceed the initial deposit before the position is closed.
  • Enter with Caution: Futures contracts often provide the option to purchase futures options, offering a less risky entry point into the market. Think of buying options as placing a deposit rather than an outright purchase. You gain the right, but not the obligation, to execute the transaction when the contract ends. Consequently, if the futures contract doesn't perform as anticipated, your loss is confined to the option's cost.
Using Stocks to Enter the Commodities Market
  • Diversified Exposure: Investors keen on specific commodities markets can opt to invest in stocks of companies operating within those sectors. For instance, those eyeing the oil industry might consider oil drilling firms, refineries, tanker companies, or diversified oil corporations. Similarly, those interested in gold can explore mining companies, smelters, or any entity involved in bullion.
  • Less Volatility: Stocks generally offer less susceptibility to volatile price fluctuations compared to futures contracts. They are more accessible to buy, hold, trade, and monitor. Moreover, investors can pinpoint their investments to specific sectors, requiring thorough research into the companies of interest.
  • Options Availability: Investors can also delve into options on commodity stocks, such as natural gas or oil companies and refineries. These options necessitate a smaller investment than directly purchasing stocks. However, while the risk for a stock option is confined to its cost, commodity price changes may not perfectly mirror the activity of a related company’s stock.
  • Accessibility and Liquidity: Investing in stocks provides the advantage of trading through a brokerage account, with readily available financial information on companies. Stocks are often highly liquid, offering ease of buying and selling compared to other commodity investment forms.
Drawbacks:
  • Diluted Exposure: Stocks do not offer a direct reflection of commodity prices, as company-related factors can influence their value independently of commodity prices.
  • External Influences: The price of a stock can be affected by factors unrelated to the commodities being tracked, posing challenges in accurately assessing commodity market movements.
